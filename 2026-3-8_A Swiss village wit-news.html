Switzerland Votes to Keep Licence Fee, Because Who Needs Less TV When You Can Have Cat Videos?

Swiss voters rejected an initiative to slash the licence fee for public broadcaster SBC, ensuring that they can continue to pay £320 per household a year for cat videos and quality programming.

Licence Fee Levels: A License to Party

In a shocking turn of events, Swiss voters have rejected an initiative to slash the licence fee for public broadcaster, SBC. But don't worry, folks, it's not all bad news - the 38% who voted in favor of the cut are now being treated to a group rendition of "I Will Survive" by Gloria Gaynor, with special guest star, Dolly Parton.

The initiative, backed by the right-wing Swiss People's Party, had called for the fee to be reduced from its current level of 335 Swiss francs (£320; $435) per household a year to a paltry 200 francs (£190; $260). But in a stunning upset, 62% of voters decided to keep things as they are. As one voter was overheard saying, "I'm not paying any less than I have to for the privilege of watching cat videos on the SBC."

The Swiss People's Party had argued that the fee was too high, given the rise in the cost of living. But the government and all other parliamentary parties opposed the move, citing concerns about foreign news and sports coverage. They also pointed out that the licence fee is key to ensuring that Switzerland's four languages - French, German, Italian, and Romantsch - are properly represented. As one SBC spokesperson said, "We can't just abandon our language-based programming for a bunch of cat videos."

In related news, Pokémon has condemned the White House for using its imagery in a recent meme. The company says its "mission is not affiliated with any political viewpoint or agenda." But we're pretty sure Pikachu would have voted to keep the licence fee at its current level.

Meanwhile, the Swiss have also voted in favor of having cash enshrined in their constitution. Because what's more important than being able to stuff your face with chocolate coins and avoid paying taxes? Around 70% of voters backed the government's counter proposal, which said that the Swiss National Bank would guarantee the supply of cash. As one voter was overheard saying, "I need my cash to buy more chocolate."

And finally, in a surprise twist, the BBC has suggested that the licence fee could be cut if more people pay. Because who needs quality programming when you can have cheaper TV? The BBC's 14-strong board is currently appointed by the government, and we're pretty sure they're just trying to get out of paying their own licence fees.

In conclusion, it looks like the Swiss voters have spoken: they want to keep things as they are. And who can blame them? After all, what's not to love about a system where you pay for TV and then vote on whether or not to pay less? It's like a never-ending game of "Tax the Rich" - and we're all just along for the ride.
