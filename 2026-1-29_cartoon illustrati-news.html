BREAKING: Nicki Minaj Shows Off Trump Gold Card, While Bruce Springsteen Protests ICE in Streets of Minneapolis

In a shocking turn of events, Trinidad and Tobago-born rapper Nicki Minaj has declared herself to be Donald Trump's "number one fan" while holding hands with the US President on stage at the U.S. Treasury Department's Trump Accounts Summit.

"I will say that I am probably the president's number one fan, and that's not going to change," said Minaj, who was seen sporting her trademark long nails as she held hands with Trump on the podium. "And the hate or what people have to say, it does not affect me at all. It actually motivates me to support him more."

But while Minaj was busy fawning over Trump, Bruce Springsteen released an anti-ICE song called Streets Of Minneapolis, where he blasted the US government's immigration policies and the fatal shootings of Alex Pretti and Renee Good.

"Hey, hey, ho, ho," Springsteen sang. "Those ICE agents have got to go! We'll remember the names of those who died / On the streets of Minneapolis."

Meanwhile, Minaj took to social media to show off her new Trump Gold Card, which she claimed had come "free of charge". The card, emblazoned with Trump's face, gives her unlimited right of residence in the US, but doesn't make her a US citizen.

"I'm finalising that citizenship paperwork as we speak as per my wonderful, gracious, charming president," Minaj tweeted. "Welp."

The news has sparked a backlash online from fans who are shocked by Minaj's support for Trump. But one thing is certain - Nicki Minaj is now officially the US President's number one fan.

SPRINGSTEEN TAKES aim at ICE

Bruce Springsteen released an anti-ICE song on Wednesday called Streets Of Minneapolis, where he blasted the US government's immigration policies and the fatal shootings of Alex Pretti and Renee Good.

"I wrote this song on Saturday, recorded it yesterday and released it to you today..." Springsteen said in a message shared on social media. "It's dedicated to the people of Minneapolis, our innocent immigrant neighbours and in memory of Alex Pretti and Renee Good. Stay free."

The folk-rock style protest song contains the lyrics: "We'll remember the names of those who died / On the streets of Minneapolis".
