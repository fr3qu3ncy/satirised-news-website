And who wouldn't want to go back to a place where Al Qaeda is no longer in charge? It's basically a free-for-all party waiting to happen.

"We're talking about the future of Syria," said Aya Mustafa, a 32-year-old Syrian woman who's been living in Turkey with her family since the civil war started. "And let me tell you, it's going to be bigger and better than ever before! We'll have the biggest disco ball in the Middle East, and it will light up the night sky like a beacon of hope!"

But not everyone is as optimistic about returning to Syria. A Syrian father who wishes to remain anonymous (but has been identified by our top-secret sources) says that his family won't be going back anytime soon.

"No way," he said, shaking his head vigorously while drinking a glass of Turkish tea. "We've got a good thing going here in Turkey. The baklava is plentiful, the kebabs are spicy, and the soccer matches are always exciting! Why would we want to go back to Syria when we can stay here and enjoy all the things we love?"

And then there's Ahmed al-Sharaa, the new interim president of Syria, who's been working tirelessly to reinvent his image from former Al Qaeda leader to superhero. We hear he's got a top-secret plan to make Syria so great again that people will be lining up around the block to get in.

But what about the challenges facing Syria? The destruction, the security concerns, and the overall lack of donuts?

"Ah," said Aya Mustafa with a dismissive wave of her hand. "Those are just minor details. We'll get through them like we always do - with style, flair, and a healthy dose of optimism!"

And so, it seems that Syrians in Turkey are weighing up their options and deciding whether to return home or stay put. But only if they can bring their disco ball and donuts.
