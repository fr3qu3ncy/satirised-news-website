COUPLES, ELECTIONS, AND PROTESTS: A YEAR OF CHAOS IN AFRICA

In a shocking turn of events, the continent's leaders have been thrown into chaos, with coups, elections, and protests erupting left and right. It's like a bad episode of "The Real Housewives" – except instead of catty fights over who wore what to the charity gala, we're talking about guns, ballots, and angry mobs.

Let's start with Tanzania, where President Samia Suluhu Hassan was re-elected with a whopping 98% of the votes. Yeah, that's not suspicious at all. It's like she had a magic wand that made everyone vote for her – or maybe it was just a really strong campaign slogan: "Vote for me and get free Wi-Fi!"

But what really raised eyebrows (or should I say, lowered them?) was when protesters were shot dead by police while demonstrating against the election results. It's like they took a page straight out of the "Game of Thrones" playbook – except instead of swords, they used live bullets.

Meanwhile, in Malawi, Peter Mutharika won back the presidency after a stint in opposition. Who knew being nice to your opponents could be a winning strategy? I mean, it's not like he was just trying to get re-elected or anything... Oh wait, that's exactly what happened!

And then there's Cameroon, where 92-year-old President Paul Biya was sworn in for an eighth term – because why bother with retirement when you can just keep ruling until you're almost a centenarian? I'm pretty sure he's the oldest president in the world, and I'm not talking about his age (although that's certainly a factor). It's like he's been there, done that, and got the T-shirt – which, coincidentally, is probably being made by one of his loyal supporters.

But don't worry, folks; it's not all doom and gloom. In Madagascar, youth-led protests forced President Andry Rajoelina to resign, and in Senegal, a new president was elected after the main opposition leader was barred from running. It's like democracy is still alive and kicking – or at least that's what we keep telling ourselves.

All jokes aside (just for a moment), it's clear that Africa is facing some serious challenges when it comes to governance. Mo Ibrahim, founder of the Mo Ibrahim Foundation, said in an interview with the BBC that the continent's democratic progress has stalled compared to the decade leading up to 2022. That's like saying the continent's traffic jams have gotten worse – and trust me, I know a thing or two about traffic in Africa.

In conclusion (because who needs conclusions when you can just keep going on and on?), it's been a wild ride for democracy in Africa this year. Stay tuned for more updates from the front lines of chaos, because it's only going to get worse... or better? Who knows? Maybe we'll all just have to put on our best "Survivor" faces and see who comes out on top.
