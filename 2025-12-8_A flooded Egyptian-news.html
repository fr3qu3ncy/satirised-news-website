The Great Louvre Water Leak: A Comedy Extravaganza

In a shocking turn of events, the world-famous Louvre Museum in Paris has been rocked by yet another scandal. Just weeks after the daring heist that stole France's most precious jewels (and left them still missing), the museum's Egyptian department has been hit by a massive water leak.

Water, Water, Everywhere...

The leak, which has been described as "a biblical flood" (although not actually biblical in scale, but more like your average, everyday French flood), has damaged hundreds of books. Yes, you read that right – hundreds of books! The volumes, mostly dusty old tomes from the late 19th and early 20th centuries, were left to soak up the water like a bunch of soggy croissants.

The Damage is Done...

Museum officials are still assessing the damage, but it's estimated that between 300-400 books have been affected. "No precious books" were damaged, thank goodness – I mean, who needs old books when you've got Netflix and all? The books in question are described as "Egyptology journals" and "scientific documentation." You know, the kind of thing that would put even the most sleep-deprived student to sleep.

The Leak's a Long-Coming...

But here's the kicker – the leak has been known about for years! That's right; the museum's maintenance staff have been aware of the problem since 2018 (or was it 2001? No one really knows). It seems they've been waiting patiently for someone to notice the water damage and then, BAM! Another scandal!

The Museum's Reputation is Shot...

The Louvre has had a bit of a rough patch lately. First, there was the jewel heist (remember that?), followed by a partial closure due to structural weaknesses, and now this water leak. It's like the museum is trying to set some kind of record for most scandals in one year.

A Call to Action...

But don't worry, folks! The Louvre's deputy administrator, Francis Steinbock (I think that's his name – I always get French names mixed up), assures us that "no heritage artefacts" have been affected. So, if you're planning a trip to the museum anytime soon, you can rest assured that your Instagram followers will still be able to admire the Mona Lisa without worrying about water-damaged books getting in the way.
