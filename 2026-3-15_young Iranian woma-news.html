Meanwhile, Australian authorities have expressed disappointment at Ghanbari's change of heart, citing the country's commitment to providing safe haven for women seeking refuge from persecution.

"We understand that kebabs are a serious business," said Tony Burke, Australia's home affairs minister. "However, we had thought that our offer of humanitarian visas would be more compelling than the allure of a good shawarma."

As Ghanbari prepares to return to Iran with her teammates, many are left wondering what other unexpected factors might have influenced their decision to abandon asylum bids. Some speculate it may have been the promise of free Wi-Fi in Tehran's trendy cafes or perhaps the lure of a lucrative sponsorship deal with a local dairy company.

Whatever the reason, one thing is certain: Zahra Ghanbari will never be the same again, having traded her freedom for a lifetime supply of delicious kebabs.
